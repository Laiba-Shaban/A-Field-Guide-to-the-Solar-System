A Field Guide to the Solar System

Eight planets, one demoted dwarf, and a lot of very extreme weather. Scroll outward from the Sun and meet the neighborhood.

57.9 million km from the Sun
Planet 1 of 8 · No moons

Mercury

The closest planet to the Sun is also, oddly, not the hottest — Venus wins that title. Mercury has no real atmosphere to trap heat, so it swings wildly between blistering days and brutal nights.

430°C days −180°C nights 88-day year

Field note — A day on Mercury (sunrise to sunrise) lasts about 176 Earth days: longer than its own year.

108.2 million km from the Sun
Planet 2 of 8 · No moons

Venus

A thick blanket of carbon dioxide traps heat so effectively that Venus is the hottest planet, hot enough to melt lead. It also spins backwards, and so slowly that its day is longer than its year.

465°C surface Rotates retrograde Crushing pressure

Field note — On Venus, the sun rises in the west — a consequence of its backwards spin.

149.6 million km from the Sun
Planet 3 of 8 · 1 moon

Earth

The only planet we've confirmed has liquid water on the surface, plate tectonics in motion, and — as far as we know — anyone home. It's a small, wet, rocky world tilted just enough to give us seasons.

71% ocean 1 large moon Only known life

Field note — Earth's molten core generates a magnetic field that deflects most solar radiation — without it, our atmosphere would slowly be stripped away.

227.9 million km from the Sun
Planet 4 of 8 · 2 moons

Mars

The red dust that gives Mars its color is iron oxide — literally rust. It's also home to Olympus Mons, a volcano nearly three times the height of Everest, and canyons that would swallow the Grand Canyon whole.

Olympus Mons: 21.9km Two tiny moons Thin CO₂ air

Field note — Mars has seasons like Earth, but each one lasts roughly twice as long, since its year is 687 Earth days.

778.5 million km from the Sun
Planet 5 of 8 · 95 moons

Jupiter

The largest planet by a wide margin — you could fit every other planet inside it with room to spare. Its Great Red Spot is a storm wider than Earth that's been raging for at least 190 years.

1,321 Earths fit inside Storm older than photography 95 known moons

Field note — Jupiter radiates more heat than it receives from the Sun, a leftover glow from the energy of its own formation.

1.43 billion km from the Sun
Planet 6 of 8 · 146 moons

Saturn

Its rings are made of billions of chunks of ice and rock, some as small as dust, some as large as a house — spread so thin that despite spanning 280,000km, they're only about 10 meters thick.

Rings ~10m thick Less dense than water 146 known moons

Field note — Saturn is the only planet in the solar system less dense than water — it would float, in theory, given a big enough bathtub.

2.87 billion km from the Sun
Planet 7 of 8 · 28 moons

Uranus

Knocked onto its side by an ancient collision, Uranus rolls around the Sun on its axis rather than spinning upright, giving it seasons that last over 20 years each.

98° axial tilt 21-year seasons Coldest atmosphere

Field note — Despite being farther from the Sun, Neptune runs hotter internally — Uranus holds the record for the coldest recorded planetary atmosphere.

4.5 billion km from the Sun
Planet 8 of 8 · 16 moons

Neptune

The windiest place in the solar system, with gusts clocked near 2,100 km/h — faster than the speed of sound on Earth. It's also the only planet found by math before it was found by telescope.

2,100 km/h winds Predicted mathematically 165-year orbit

Field note — Neptune has completed only one full orbit since its discovery in 1846 — it finished its first lap in 2011.

5.9 billion km from the Sun
Dwarf planet · the demoted one · 5 moons

Pluto

Reclassified in 2006 once astronomers found other objects its size in the same neighborhood. It's still got a heart-shaped glacier of nitrogen ice the size of Texas and Oklahoma combined.

Reclassified 2006 Heart-shaped glacier 248-year orbit

Field note — Pluto's largest moon, Charon, is so big relative to Pluto that the two orbit a shared point in space between them.

That's the neighborhood.

Eight planets, one very stubborn dwarf, and a Sun that accounts for 99.86% of all the mass in the system.
